"Growing olives in the Netherlands.." if you ask any Dutch whether that's a good idea, they would answer it's completely ridiculous. Not for nothing the Dutch refer to their own country as "koude kikkerlandje", literally "cold frogland", as the sea climate is often wet and cold. It's also lush, green and beautiful.

It's hard not to appreciate the warmth and the extra hours of sunlight due to the changing climate. Compared to the current 20°C on an average summer day, an increase of 2° or 5°C yearly doesn't sound that bad. Right?

The olive case study

To investigate what 2°, 4°, or 5°C warming really mean, for the Netherlands, I studied the future range in olive distribution in Europe. Just to see what happens. Olives thrive with heat, sun, drought, in a landscape that is most different from where I live. Will the Dutch exchange their landscapes for this mediterranean sight? And if so, when? 100, 200 years.. or sooner?

Picking olive plots

Using satellite images from google maps, I identified 14 locations around the mediteranean. 9 were in the middle of olive fields - and on 5 no olives were grown.


From each location, I collected climate data from the past 30 years to see whether there are differences between the plots with and without olives. Is there a minimum temperature for olive trees to grow? Is there a minimum or maximum amount of rainfall required?


Here is the temperature for the locations with and without olives. The area represents the min and max values that I found in the locations. Look how on any given time, the warmest plot without olives is just always colder than the coldest spot where I still found olives. Thus, temperature is an important factor to determine where olives grow!

On the plot you see the temperature, average per month in the period 1996-2016. The line represents the mean of the plots with and without olives, the area represents the min and max temperature of those plots.


I studied some more parameters and found that you can seperate the plots on their annual average temparure of 14 degrees; and that olives grow in areas that receive about 350-950mm precipitation yearly.

Verification

With these settings, I went to the geographical data from Worldclim.org and selected areas that have the right temperature and precipitation for olives. Just to see whether the range I select this way actaully fits the known current distribution of olives..


you can see for yourself!




The olive/green area is where olives grow according to my model.


I compared my range to the image I found on Wikipedia (if you have the original data, especially as shape file, I am very interested to overlay them directly and get real number out! Please let me know!) and found them to be surpiringly similar. Biology & cultural aspects are way more complicated, but with just temperature and rainfall we can predict quite well where olive trees grow. My model is a bit more conservative than the true olive distribution, but I like it like that. It means that it won't tell me all places that are suitable for olive trees, but at least it won't falsely mark areas as suitable if they are not.
